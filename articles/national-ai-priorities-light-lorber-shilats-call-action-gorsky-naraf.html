National AI priorities in light of Lorber and Shilat's call for action

Created on 2025-01-17 18:24

Published on 2025-01-18 23:00

Hadas Lorber and Yaron Shilat ’s insightful opinion piece, “We need to unlock Israel's AI potential” (“Jerusalem Post”, 30/12/24) underscores the immense potential of AI to transform our economy and society. They highlight the prospect of AI adding $11.4 billion to Israel's economy in 2023 — a significant result that demands urgent attention from Israeli policymakers and innovators. By analyzing key challenges and proposing actionable steps, the publication serves as a rallying call to leverage AI for advancing Israeli growth. Building on their foundation, I aim to explore how we can focus AI initiatives to maximize their impact on everyday life for Israeli citizens, especially in ways that directly improve people's lives.

Why we need to act now

Lorber and Shilat highlight that Israel is a world leader in AI skills, yet only 9.1% of Israeli companies use advanced AI. This gap between our capabilities and actual use of AI needs urgent attention. Meanwhile, the Israeli government allocates most of its budget to three main areas: education, healthcare, and city services, which together account for 63% of the 484 billion shekel budget. These sectors present a massive opportunity—even minor improvements through AI could enhance the lives of millions of Israelis.

To illustrate the potential impact, consider how AI-driven educational tools could personalize learning experiences for Israeli students, improving outcomes by up to 30%, according to a 2024 OECD report. In city services, AI-powered traffic management systems could reduce congestion by 25%, significantly improving daily commutes. Meanwhile, AI-enabled hospital workflows could cut patient wait times by 50%, according to recent studies. Prioritizing projects in these areas ensures immediate benefits for individuals while setting a strong foundation for broader AI adoption.

Training our future AI professionals

While Israel has gained global recognition as a "startup nation," its performance in international assessments like PISA and TIMSS reveals challenges in core subjects such as math and science. Despite these gaps, Israel’s innovative spirit and entrepreneurial culture have driven technological advancements, making the development of a robust AI education system both necessary and achievable. To ensure that AI’s potential is fully realized, the country must develop professionals across four interconnected education tracks.

Adult and professional education provides opportunities for reskilling and upskilling, enabling the existing workforce to adapt to AI-driven transformations. This track focuses on three distinct types of learners. The first type includes users of AI, who develop an intuition for AI systems, understand their basic principles, and learn how to effectively use AI tools and features in their respective fields. The second type includes AI system developers, who build pipelines and software by utilizing libraries, frameworks, and API calls. These professionals play a critical role in integrating AI into existing workflows and creating scalable solutions. The third type includes AI model developers, who specialize in engineering, hosting, fine-tuning, and distilling models to address specific challenges. Programs could include online courses, boot camps, and certification programs tailored to the varying needs of these groups. By addressing this spectrum of learners, Israel can ensure that its workforce remains competitive and capable of driving innovation across industries.

Educators are a critical link in this chain, requiring tailored training to harness AI tools effectively in classrooms while fostering innovation. Teachers must understand how to incorporate adaptive learning platforms and AI-driven assessment tools to enhance their teaching methods. Professional development programs focused on AI can empower educators to better engage students and personalize instruction. Moreover, educators who are well-versed in AI can serve as mentors, inspiring students to explore advanced technologies.

Universities and colleges play a pivotal role in preparing students for specialized AI careers and must cater to three distinct streams of learners. For non-computer science majors, such as those in business, law, or healthcare, the focus should be on learning how to effectively use AI tools and systems, developing intuition about their functionality, and understanding features at a basic level to enhance their professional practice. Computer science majors, on the other hand, should be equipped to become AI system developers, gaining the skills to build AI pipelines, integrate APIs, and use frameworks to implement AI in diverse applications. Finally, data science majors are poised to take on the role of AI model developers, specializing in advanced topics such as model engineering, hosting, fine-tuning, and optimization to tackle complex challenges. By tailoring educational programs to these distinct needs, universities can ensure that graduates are well-prepared to contribute effectively to the AI ecosystem.

High schools serve as the foundation for AI education, offering a general basis for all students to become proficient users of AI. This universal program would ensure that every student gains a basic understanding of how AI works, its applications, and its limitations. This foundational knowledge would empower them to use AI tools intuitively and responsibly in various fields. For a select group of students with a demonstrated aptitude and interest in technology, high schools could introduce specialized programs focusing on AI system development. These programs would provide hands-on experience with programming, algorithmic thinking, and using AI frameworks, preparing students for advanced studies and technical careers. This dual approach ensures broad accessibility while nurturing specialized talent among the most motivated students.

Practical initiatives like establishing AI education labs in high schools have already shown promise globally. For example, research published by the World Economic Forum highlights how schools in Estonia implemented AI-based tools to monitor student progress and customize teaching plans. This approach led to a measurable increase in engagement and test scores, particularly for students who were previously struggling. Programs like these demonstrate the potential for adapting international successes to the Israeli context, where such systems could address existing educational disparities while promoting innovation.

Using our resources wisely

The government’s budget allocation tells a story of priorities, reflecting the fundamental commitment of a democratic government like Israel’s to prioritize the well-being of its citizens. Ensuring access to quality education, healthcare, and efficient city services underscores the role of AI as a transformative tool to enhance lives and strengthen public trust in these critical sectors. Education receives 148.9 billion shekels (30.8%), healthcare gets 97.83 billion shekels (20.2%), and city services account for 57 billion shekels (11.8%). These figures highlight areas where AI can make the greatest difference.

In education, personalized AI tutoring systems can close achievement gaps and support teachers by automating administrative tasks. For example, an AI-powered learning assistant deployed in rural schools in India showed a 25% improvement in math scores. Similarly, in city services, smart waste management systems like those used in Singapore reduced landfill contributions by 40%, demonstrating scalable success. Healthcare administration can benefit from AI systems that streamline appointment scheduling, saving patients and providers time.

Investing in these areas not only improves public services but also creates scalable solutions that private companies can adopt, fostering a virtuous cycle of innovation. Moreover, focusing on these sectors directly impacts people’s quality of life, ensuring that AI’s benefits are widely felt.

Bringing AI efficiency

A significant challenge lies in the stark disparity between salaries in the tech sector and those in essential public services like education and healthcare. While tech professionals earn an average of 30,126 shekels per month, workers in education and healthcare earn around 7,500 shekels. This gap reflects the government’s constrained ability to allocate resources effectively across critical sectors. As a result, the integration of AI is no longer optional but essential to improve efficiency, reduce resource wastage, and achieve more with limited budgets.

AI can help public services deliver better outcomes without a proportional increase in manpower. For instance, in education, AI-driven systems can automate administrative tasks, create personalized learning pathways, and identify at-risk students early for intervention. Similarly, in healthcare, predictive analytics and AI-powered diagnostic tools can optimize patient care, freeing up resources for critical areas. These technologies not only enhance service quality but also allow for reallocation of funds to raise salaries for essential workers, addressing the disparity in compensation.

To attract talent and ensure the sustainability of these improvements, public sector roles must be reimagined. These positions should offer competitive compensation, professional growth opportunities, and the chance to work on cutting-edge projects with significant societal impact. Establishing innovation hubs where academia, government, and industry collaborate on AI solutions for public services can further bridge the talent gap. Such initiatives would demonstrate how AI can transform essential services while providing skilled professionals with meaningful career paths.

By leveraging AI to enhance efficiency and align resources with the priorities of a democratic society, Israel can ensure its public services remain effective, sustainable, and focused on improving the well-being of its citizens.

Another approach is incentivizing collaboration between academia, industry, and government. For instance, an innovation hub focused on AI applications in education could host competitions where university teams develop tools for public schools. Such initiatives would not only advance technology but also highlight the tangible impact of AI on society.

Building a new AI ecosystem

Building a robust AI ecosystem in Israel requires a foundational shift that aligns with the recommendations in Lorber and Shilat's article, "We need to unlock Israel's AI potential." They emphasize that democratizing AI tools and creating accessible infrastructure are essential first steps. This perspective underscores the necessity of integrating talent development, infrastructure expansion, and collaborative partnerships into a cohesive framework.

One example of best practices comes from Finland’s AI strategy, which includes the "Elements of AI" initiative—a free online course designed to educate citizens about AI fundamentals. This initiative has reached hundreds of thousands of people, creating widespread awareness and competency in using AI. Israel can adopt a similar approach, offering localized training programs tailored to the needs of diverse communities and industries.

The foundation of Israel’s ecosystem must focus on talent development. Universities, vocational programs, and research institutions should prioritize AI-specific training across disciplines. This approach ensures a workforce that includes system developers, model engineers, and professionals skilled in applying AI solutions in non-technical sectors. Interdisciplinary collaboration—such as between technologists and healthcare professionals—is essential for impactful solutions.

Infrastructure forms the second pillar of this ecosystem. To democratize AI, Israel must expand access to computational resources like cloud platforms and GPU clusters, especially for startups and small businesses. Public-private partnerships can play a critical role here, enabling shared resources that allow innovators to experiment and scale.

The third critical component is collaboration. Strong partnerships between government, industry, and academia are vital for fostering innovation. Innovation hubs—spaces where researchers, entrepreneurs, and policymakers converge—can drive breakthroughs in sectors ranging from agriculture to public administration. Additionally, regulatory sandboxes tailored to Israel’s context can accelerate the safe deployment of AI in sensitive areas such as healthcare and education.

By adopting these best practices and focusing on the pillars of talent, infrastructure, and collaboration, Israel can create an AI ecosystem that not only drives economic growth but also addresses pressing societal challenges. This holistic approach ensures AI advancements are inclusive, sustainable, and aligned with the country’s long-term goals.

To encourage experimentation, Israel should expand its use of regulatory sandboxes. These controlled environments allow companies to test innovative AI solutions while ensuring compliance with legal and ethical standards. For example, a dedicated sandbox for education could fast-track the development of AI tools that align with national curricula while addressing privacy concerns.

Building an ecosystem requires collaboration at all levels. Universities should partner with startups and established tech firms to create multidisciplinary AI centers. These hubs would not only foster innovation but also serve as training grounds for the next generation of AI leaders. Similarly, government incentives, such as tax breaks for companies developing socially impactful AI projects, can stimulate investment in underserved sectors.

What we can do next

To ensure that AI benefits every citizen, Israel must take a multifaceted approach that addresses education, municipal services, and infrastructure, while fostering international collaboration.

School education must be the cornerstone of Israel’s AI strategy at the foundational level. Schools should integrate AI tools into the curriculum to ensure that every student gains familiarity with the technology. For instance, school chatbots can support personalized learning by helping students with homework, answering questions, and tracking progress. These tools empower students to engage with AI in practical, intuitive ways, fostering early competence and curiosity. Specialized programs for high-achieving students could further include coding basics, algorithmic thinking, and exposure to AI development frameworks, ensuring a pipeline of talent for advanced AI roles.

University education builds on this foundation by advancing the use and development of AI at a higher academic level. Universities must implement AI-powered systems that improve both learning and administration. University chatbots, for example, can streamline processes like enrollment, academic advising, and career guidance while providing tailored support to students. Additionally, universities should emphasize hands-on research and collaboration with industries to develop cutting-edge AI technologies. Specific streams for computer science and data science majors could focus on pipeline development, model engineering, and ethical considerations, ensuring graduates are ready to contribute effectively to Israel's AI ecosystem.

Municipal services present another critical opportunity for AI adoption. Developing AI-centered customer service environments for municipalities can streamline citizen interactions. For example, chatbots could handle routine inquiries, freeing up staff to address complex cases. Additionally, predictive analytics could optimize resource allocation for services such as waste management, transportation, and utilities, making cities more efficient and responsive.

Infrastructure for AI must be democratized to ensure equitable access. This includes providing API credits and GPU clusters to educational institutions, enabling them to integrate advanced AI tools into their programs. Establishing centralized GPU resources for municipal governments can further enhance their ability to deploy AI solutions cost-effectively.

International collaboration is essential for leveraging global expertise and resources. Israel should partner with leading AI nations to share knowledge, tools, and best practices. Joint research initiatives and cross-border innovation hubs could position Israel as a global leader in AI-driven solutions, while ensuring alignment with ethical and regulatory standards.

By focusing on these strategic areas, Israel can create a sustainable and inclusive AI ecosystem that addresses both current challenges and future opportunities. This vision aligns with the nation’s commitment to innovation and the well-being of its citizens, ensuring that AI advancements contribute to a brighter and more equitable future for all.

Lorber and Shilat’s article provides a powerful vision for harnessing AI to unlock Israel’s potential. Building on their foundation, this expanded approach emphasizes the importance of integrating AI into education, municipal services, and infrastructure while fostering international collaboration. By adopting these strategies, Israel can not only achieve the transformative goals outlined by Lorber and Shilat but also set a global example of innovation, inclusivity, and societal impact driven by AI. The time to act is now, ensuring that every citizen benefits from the advancements this technology can bring.
